Bottom Line

Phenom 300 (EMB-505) is the clear reliability winner. Its 928 SDRs sound alarming until you normalize by fleet size (~700 aircraft) — that's roughly 0.33 SDRs per aircraft per year, and the majority are routine emergency lighting battery swaps. Only 13% of events resulted in an unscheduled landing. NetJets and Wheels Up run these jets at near-98% dispatch reliability in high-cycle operations. The PW617F engine has an excellent track record and the maintenance network is the largest of the three.

Pilatus PC-24 is a capable aircraft with a known 2022 cabin heater epidemic that Pilatus addressed via service bulletins. The LPT nozzle destruction and recurring flap/brake/gear events are concerning for a small fleet of ~120 aircraft. Improving trajectory but best suited for operators with dedicated maintenance support and factory agreements. Don't buy an early production airframe without a thorough pre-buy.

Cirrus SF50 Vision Jet has the smallest fleet and the most alarming incident profile. A CAPS deployment due to autothrottle failure, repeated FADEC events, a fleet-wide distributor housing defect affecting 13+ aircraft simultaneously, dual fuel valve failures, and cascading CAS events at FL310 paint a picture of an immature platform with systemic issues. The trend is improving (20 SDRs in 2022 → 2 in 2025) but the aircraft's single-engine dependency leaves zero margin when things go wrong. Unless you have a strong maintenance contract and alternate jet access, this is a significant operational risk.
